Stupas, Viharas, Chaityas & Sculptures

üìñ Introduction

Buddhism (founded ~6th century BC by Gautama Buddha) profoundly influenced Indian art and architecture.

  • Buddhist art emerged during the Mauryan period (especially under Emperor Ashoka)
  • Major forms: Stupas, Viharas, Chaityas, Pillars, Sculptures
  • Initially aniconic (no direct image of Buddha) ‚Üí Later iconic (anthropomorphic Buddha images)

üïâÔ∏è Stupas Super PYQ

What is a Stupa? A hemispherical dome-shaped structure containing Buddhist relics.
Purpose: Commemorative monument, meditation site, pilgrimage center

üìê Components of a Stupa:

Component Description
Anda Hemispherical dome (main body)
Harmika Square railing on top of dome
Yashti (Chhatravali) Central pillar with umbrellas symbolizing honor
Vedika Railing/fence around the stupa
Torana Ornamental gateway (usually 4 directions)
Medhi Circular terrace for circumambulation (Pradakshina)

üèõÔ∏è Famous Stupas: Super PYQ

  • Sanchi Stupa (Madhya Pradesh) UNESCO World Heritage
    • Commissioned by Emperor Ashoka (3rd century BC)
    • Enlarged during Shunga dynasty
    • Famous for 4 ornate Toranas with intricate carvings
    • Depicts Jataka tales (Buddha's previous births)
    • No direct image of Buddha (aniconic phase)
  • Bharhut Stupa (Madhya Pradesh)
    • Built during Shunga period
    • Famous for narrative sculptures
  • Amaravati Stupa (Andhra Pradesh)
    • Built during Satavahana dynasty
    • Famous for white marble carvings
  • Dhamek Stupa (Sarnath, UP)
    • Marks the spot where Buddha gave his first sermon

üõï Chaityas & Viharas PYQ

1Ô∏è‚É£ Chaitya (Prayer Hall):

  • Buddhist prayer hall with a stupa inside
  • Usually rock-cut (carved from solid rock)
  • Horseshoe-shaped entrance (Chaitya window)
  • Apsidal (semi-circular) plan
  • Famous Example: Karla Chaitya (Maharashtra) - largest rock-cut Chaitya

2Ô∏è‚É£ Vihara (Monastery):

  • Buddhist monastery for monks to reside
  • Central courtyard surrounded by cells (kutis)
  • Used for meditation, study, living
  • Famous Examples: Ajanta Caves, Ellora Caves
üí° Difference: Chaitya = Prayer hall with stupa | Vihara = Residential monastery

üèîÔ∏è Rock-Cut Buddhist Caves Super PYQ

üé® Ajanta Caves (Maharashtra) UNESCO World Heritage

  • Total 30 caves (5th-6th century AD)
  • Contains both Chaityas and Viharas
  • Famous for Buddhist paintings (frescoes)
  • Depicts: Jataka tales, Buddha's life, Bodhisattvas
  • Cave 1: Padmapani and Vajrapani Bodhisattva paintings
  • Cave 26: Mahaparinirvana (reclining Buddha)

üóø Ellora Caves (Maharashtra) UNESCO World Heritage

  • Total 34 caves (Buddhist, Hindu, Jain)
  • Cave 10 (Vishwakarma Cave): Famous Buddhist Chaitya
  • Multi-religious complex (unique feature)

üîÑ Aniconic vs Iconic Representation PYQ

Aniconic Phase Iconic Phase
No direct image of Buddha Anthropomorphic Buddha images
Buddha represented by symbols: Buddha depicted in human form
- Bodhi Tree (Enlightenment) Developed in Gandhara & Mathura schools
- Dharma Chakra (Wheel) Shows Buddha in various mudras (gestures)
- Footprints (Paduka) Influences: Greco-Roman (Gandhara), Indian (Mathura)
- Empty throne Later spread to entire Buddhist world
- Stupa Gupta period: Refined & idealized Buddha images

üî£ Buddhist Symbolism

  • Bodhi Tree: Enlightenment
  • Dharma Chakra (Wheel): Buddha's first sermon (Dhammachakra Pravartan)
  • Lotus: Purity, spiritual awakening
  • Empty Throne: Buddha's royal renunciation
  • Footprints: Buddha's presence
  • Stupa: Buddha's Parinirvana (final liberation)
  • Lion: Royal power, Buddha's clan (Shakya)
  • Deer: Buddha's first sermon at Sarnath (Deer Park)
