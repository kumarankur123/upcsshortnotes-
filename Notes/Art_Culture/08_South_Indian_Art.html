Pallava, Chola & Bronze Mastery

üìñ Introduction

South Indian art reached its zenith under the Pallava (6th-9th century) and Chola (9th-13th century) dynasties.

  • Pallavas: Initiated rock-cut architecture
  • Cholas: Perfected bronze sculpture (Lost-wax technique)
  • Both dynasties contributed to temple architecture and stone sculptures

üëë Chola Art (9th-13th Century AD) Super PYQ

üèØ Chola Temple Architecture:

  • Brihadeeswarar Temple (Thanjavur) UNESCO
    • Built by Rajaraja Chola I (1010 AD)
    • Tallest Vimana in India (66 meters / 216 feet)
    • Monolithic Nandi (13 feet high, carved from single stone)
    • Pyramidal tower with 13 tiers
    • Entire temple made from granite
  • Gangaikonda Cholapuram Temple
    • Built by Rajendra Chola I
    • Similar to Thanjavur, slightly smaller

ü•â Chola Bronze Sculptures Super PYQ - Most Important

Technique: Lost-Wax (Cire-perdue) Method
1. Wax model created
2. Clay mold covers wax
3. Wax melted & drained out
4. Molten bronze poured into hollow mold
5. After cooling, clay mold broken to reveal bronze sculpture

üïâÔ∏è Nataraja - Symbolism: Super PYQ

  • 4 Arms:
    • Upper right: Damaru (drum) = Creation sound
    • Upper left: Agni (fire) = Destruction
    • Lower right: Abhaya Mudra = Protection
    • Lower left: Points to raised left foot = Liberation
  • Right leg: Crushing demon Apasmara (ignorance)
  • Left leg: Raised (represents liberation)
  • Ring of Fire (Pra bhavali): Cosmic energy
  • Jata (Hair): Flowing locks with Ganga river
